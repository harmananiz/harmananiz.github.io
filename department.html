University of Toronto: The Institute of Communication, Culture, Information and Technology Website


Introduction

This case study focuses on improving the user experience of the ICCIT department website. The goal is to address concerns related to an overwhelming interface, confusing navigation, information overload, and lack of prioritization for first-year students. The study aims to create a more intuitive and user-friendly website for the target audience.


Research and Analysis

During the research phase, user observations were conducted to gain insights into how users interacted with the website. The observations revealed key concerns such as an excessive amount of information, unclear information architecture, and the presence of multiple navigation bars. Additionally, the study identified redundant elements like carousels and numerous links, contributing to information overload. The lack of prioritization for first-year students and potential cultural constraints caused by non-universally recognized symbols were also noted.


Objectives

The objectives of the case study were to simplify the website interface and streamline the information architecture to reduce cognitive load. The study aimed to consolidate the multiple navigation bars into a cohesive and user-friendly navigation system. It also sought to remove or reorganize redundant elements, such as carousels and excessive links, to declutter the website. Prioritizing content and resources relevant to first-year students was another goal, aiming to improve usability. Additionally, the study aimed to ensure the use of universally recognized symbols and icons for better cross-cultural communication.

Solutions and Implementation

To address the identified concerns, the website interface was redesigned to create a cleaner and more visually appealing design. The multiple navigation bars were consolidated into a single, intuitive navigation system to simplify user navigation. Redundant elements, like carousels, were removed or adjusted to simplify information presentation. Content was restructured, and priority was given to information relevant to first-year students. Non-universally recognized symbols were replaced with globally understood visual elements to promote inclusivity.


Results and Impact

The implemented solutions resulted in an improved user experience on the ICCIT department website. The redesigned interface provided a less overwhelming and more intuitive experience for users. The simplified navigation system allowed users to find relevant information more easily. By removing redundant elements and optimizing content presentation, the study reduced information overload. Prioritizing content for first-year students met their specific needs, resulting in improved engagement. The use of universally recognized symbols avoided cultural constraints and enhanced cross-cultural communication.


Conclusion

This study successfully addressed the concerns of the ICCIT department website by implementing user-centered design principles. The redesigned interface, simplified navigation, optimized content, and globally understood symbols resulted in an improved user experience for first-year students. The case study highlights the significance of prioritizing user needs and streamlining information to create a more effective and user-friendly website.
